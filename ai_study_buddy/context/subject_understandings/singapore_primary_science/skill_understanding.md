## Singapore Primary Science — Skill Understanding (draft)

**Purpose:** This document captures the **skill dimension** of Singapore Primary Science questions, to complement the **concept dimension** captured in `syllabus_understanding.md`.

- **Concept axis**: *WHAT* science idea the question is about — tagged using  
  `Theme > Chapter > Topic` from the syllabus (see the index table in `syllabus_understanding.md`).
- **Skill axis**: *HOW* the student must think about that idea to answer — tagged using the taxonomy below.

Each question should normally have **one concept tag** and **one primary skill tag**. Cross‑cutting flags (e.g. `Experiment > Fair-test`) can be added as extra skill tags when clearly in play.

---

## Skill taxonomy (question-level)

### 1. Skill > Recall

- **Definition**: Direct retrieval of a fact, definition, label, or simple relationship with minimal reasoning.
- **Typical stems**: “State…”, “Name…”, “Give one example of…”, “What is the function of X?”
- **Examples**:
  - Name the part labelled X.
  - State one function of the roots.

### 2. Skill > Explanatory reasoning

- **Definition**: Explaining an observation, pattern, or phenomenon by linking one or more science concepts in a logical way.
- **Typical stems**: “Explain why…”, “Why did…”, “Use scientific ideas to explain…”
- **Examples**:
  - Explain why metal feels colder than plastic.
  - Explain why a shorter plank makes it harder to push the box up.

### 3. Skill > Data / Graph / Table interpretation

- **Definition**: Reading, describing, or drawing conclusions from **data representations** such as tables, bar charts, line graphs, or multi‑column observations.
- **Typical stems**: “Using the graph/table, which…”, “Describe the relationship between…”, “From the data, what can you conclude…”
- **Examples**:
  - Use the height–fertiliser table to describe how plant height changes.
  - From the graph, state how temperature affects the rate of photosynthesis.

### 4. Skill > Classification & comparison

- **Definition**: Grouping or distinguishing items based on characteristics; comparing similarities and differences.
- **Typical stems**: “Which group does X belong to…”, “Compare…”, “Which statement correctly describes…”
- **Examples**:
  - Identify whether the organisms form a population or community.
  - Choose the correct statement comparing complete and incomplete metamorphosis.

### 5. Skill > Prediction / extrapolation

- **Definition**: Predicting what will happen in a new or extended situation using known patterns or rules.
- **Typical stems**: “What will happen if…”, “Predict and explain…”, “Which option best shows…”
- **Examples**:
  - Predict the temperature after two hours.
  - Predict which circuit arrangement will give the brightest bulb.

### 6. Skill > Experiment > Fair-test

- **Definition**: Designing, critiquing, or recognising a **fair test** — including manipulated (changed) variables, measured variables, control variables, and valid/invalid comparisons.
- **Typical stems**: “Which set‑up is a fair test…”, “Identify two variables to keep the same…”, “Explain why this is/not a fair comparison…”
- **Tagging**:
  - Use as a **primary skill tag** when fair‑test reasoning is the main demand.
  - Use as an **additional skill tag** when a question both interprets data and explicitly asks about fairness.
- **Examples**:
  - Identify which experiment is fair.
  - State two variables that must be kept the same.

### 7. Skill > Experimental conclusion / evidence use

- **Definition**: Drawing a **science conclusion** from an experiment or set of observations, and linking it back to the question or hypothesis.
- **Typical stems**: “What can you conclude from…”, “Do the results support the hypothesis…”, “Which plant should she choose; explain using the results…”
- **Examples**:
  - Decide which plant Karen should buy based on flowering times and bee visitation.
  - Conclude whether fertiliser beyond a certain amount helps or harms growth.

### 8. Skill > Procedural understanding (apparatus / set-up)

- **Definition**: Understanding how a set‑up works (apparatus, circuits, arrangements) without strongly emphasising fair‑test design.
- **Typical stems**: “Which circuit will light…”, “Explain why the bulb does not light…”, “Why is the thermometer placed as shown…”
- **Examples**:
  - Decide which circuit boards complete a circuit.
  - Explain why adding a second rubber band makes the plane fly higher.

## Tagging guidelines

### Concept tag (WHAT)

- Always use a **syllabus-aligned** tag from the index in `syllabus_understanding.md`:
  - Format: `Theme > Chapter > Topic`
  - Use `—` for Topic when the syllabus unit has no separate topic name.
- When a question uses content mainly as a setting (e.g. fertiliser vs plant height) but clearly sits inside a unit, still tag that unit as the concept.

### Skill tag (HOW)

- Choose **one primary skill** from this taxonomy that best describes the cognitive demand.
- Allow **additional skill tags** only when:
  - There is a clearly separable cross‑cutting skill (e.g. `Experiment > Fair-test`).
  - The question explicitly requires two distinct moves (e.g. interpret graph **and** check fair test).

### Examples

- “State the meaning of pollination.”  
  - Concept: `Cycles > Cycles in Plants and Animals > Reproduction`  
  - Skill: `Skill > Recall`

- “From the table, describe how plant height changes as fertiliser increases.”  
  - Concept: `Systems > Plant System > Plant parts and functions` (plant growth under fertiliser)  
  - Skill: `Skill > Data / Graph / Table interpretation`

- “Which set‑up is a fair test to compare boiling times; explain your choice.”  
  - Concept: `Energy > Energy Forms and Uses > Heat`  
  - Skill: `Skill > Experiment > Fair-test`
