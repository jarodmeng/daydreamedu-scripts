This document is a topic-level summary derived from reading the *Science PSLE Revision Guide*. Topics follow the chapter list in `Science PSLE Revision Guide - 01 - Preface and Table of Contents` (page 4).

Use this as a growing knowledge base:
- Add concise mastery notes under each topic.

## Diversity

### Theme understanding

`Diversity` is about recognizing that living things and materials come in many forms, and that these differences can be observed, compared, and used for classification.

Across chapters 1 to 6, students should build these big ideas:
- things can be grouped according to observable characteristics
- living things can be distinguished from non-living things using characteristics of life
- plants, animals, fungi, and bacteria each have defining features and important internal variety
- materials differ in their properties, and those properties affect how they are used

Students should gradually see that diversity is not just about noticing that things are different. It is about describing differences precisely, choosing useful criteria for comparison, and using those criteria to organize understanding.

Common cross-chapter habits of thinking in this theme:
- look for the characteristic being used, not just the object being named
- compare things within the same category before jumping across categories
- avoid overgeneralizing from one example to the whole group
- remember that useful classification depends on evidence from characteristics, functions, or properties

This theme lays the groundwork for later science topics because students first need to recognize patterns, similarities, differences, and suitable groupings before they can reason well about systems, cycles, and interactions.

### 1. Classifying Things

#### Big Questions / intended mastery

Students should be able to answer these clearly:
- What is classification?
- Why do we classify things?
- How can the same set of things be classified in different ways?

#### Core understanding

- Classification is the process of putting things into groups.
- We classify things based on their characteristics.
- Classification helps us notice similarities and differences clearly.
- Things with similar characteristics are grouped together, while things with different characteristics are placed in different groups.

#### Why classification matters

- Classification helps us understand the world in a more organized way.
- It makes it easier to identify, compare, and describe things.
- It also helps us relate a newly observed thing to things we already know.

Students should see classification as a thinking tool, not just a sorting activity.

#### How things can be classified

- Things can be classified using tables, diagrams, and branching groupings.
- The same collection of objects can be classified in more than one valid way.
- The choice of grouping depends on the characteristic being used.

Useful characteristics in this chapter:
- living or non-living
- shape
- colour
- size

Key idea students should internalize:
- classification depends on the rule chosen
- if the rule changes, the groups can change too

#### Living and non-living as a first major classification

- One common way to classify things is into living things and non-living things.
- Living things and non-living things belong to different groups because they have different characteristics.
- Students should understand that this grouping uses whether something is living as the classification rule.

#### Reading and creating simple classification diagrams

- Classification diagrams show how one big group can be split into smaller groups.
- A broad group such as `Things` can first be divided into `Living things` and `Non-living things`.
- Living things can then be further classified by characteristics such as body covering, how they reproduce, and how they move.
- Non-living things can be further classified by characteristics such as shape, colour, texture, or the properties of the materials they are made of.

This helps students see that classification can happen in stages, from broad groups to more specific groups.

#### What students should truly master from this topic

By the end of this topic, students should be able to:
- state that classification means grouping things according to characteristics
- explain why classification is useful for understanding and organizing information
- classify a set of objects according to a named characteristic
- explain that the same objects can be classified in different valid ways
- read and interpret simple classification tables and diagrams

#### Common assessment traps

- treating classification as matching by appearance only rather than by a stated characteristic
- assuming there is only one correct way to classify a set of things
- mixing up the objects being grouped with the characteristic used to group them
- giving groups without naming the rule used

### 2. Living And Non-living Things

#### Big Questions / intended mastery

Students should be able to answer these clearly:
- What are living things and non-living things?
- What characteristics make something living?
- What are some broad groups of living things?
- How can non-living things be classified?

#### Core understanding

- Things around us can be grouped into living things and non-living things.
- Living things are alive.
- Non-living things are not alive.
- Some non-living things are made from materials that once came from living things, but that does not make the objects themselves living.

Important distinction:
- something made from wood, wool, or another material from a living thing can still be non-living

#### Characteristics of living things

Living things:
- need air, water, and food to survive
- grow
- can move by themselves, either as a whole organism or by moving body parts
- respond to changes in their surroundings
- reproduce

Non-living things:
- do not need air, water, or food
- do not grow
- do not move by themselves
- do not respond to changes in their surroundings
- do not reproduce

Students should treat these characteristics as the main test for deciding whether something is living or non-living.

#### Movement and response: two easy places to get confused

- Animals can move from place to place to look for food and shelter.
- Plants usually cannot move from place to place, but parts of plants can move.
- Plants also respond to changes around them, so they are living things.
- A thing that moves is not necessarily living; it may simply be pushed or pulled.

Common examples worth remembering:
- a mimosa plant closes its leaves when touched
- a car can move, but it is non-living because it does not move by itself in the biological sense and does not show the other characteristics of life

#### Reproduction and survival

- Living things reproduce so their kind continues to exist.
- If living things fail to reproduce, their kind may eventually disappear.
- When a type of living thing no longer exists, it is extinct.

This chapter introduces extinction only at a simple awareness level, not as a deep ecology topic.

#### Broad groups of living things

- Living things can be classified into plants, animals, fungi, and bacteria.

Useful broad distinctions:
- plants make their own food
- animals eat other living things for food
- fungi get food from dead, decaying, or waste matter

Movement differences across groups:
- many animals move from place to place
- plants can move parts of themselves but not from place to place
- fungi can move certain parts by themselves but generally do not move from place to place

#### Classifying non-living things

- Non-living things can be classified according to characteristics such as shape, colour, and texture.
- They can also be classified according to the properties of the materials they are made of.

Examples of material properties highlighted in the chapter:
- strength
- flexibility
- ability to float or sink in water

#### What students should truly master from this topic

By the end of this topic, students should be able to:
- distinguish living things from non-living things using characteristics of life
- explain why plants are living things even though they usually do not move from place to place
- explain why a moving object is not automatically living
- identify the broad groups plants, animals, fungi, and bacteria
- classify non-living things by observable characteristics or material properties

#### Common assessment traps

- saying plants are non-living because they do not walk or move from place to place
- saying anything that moves is living
- treating objects made from wood, wool, or leather as living things
- listing only one characteristic of living things instead of using several together
- confusing food source or movement examples with the defining characteristics of life

### 3. Plants

#### Big Questions / intended mastery

Students should be able to answer these clearly:
- What are the main characteristics of plants?
- Where can plants grow?
- How can plants be classified?
- What are the different parts of a plant?

#### Core understanding

- Plants are living things.
- Like other living things, they need air, water, and food to survive.
- Plants can make their own food.
- There are many different kinds of plants, and they come in different shapes, sizes, and colours.

Students should connect this topic to the earlier chapter on living things:
- plants are living even though they usually do not move from place to place
- the ability to make their own food is an important feature that distinguishes plants from animals

#### Why plants matter in daily life

- Many plants are useful to people because they provide food, shelter, medicines, and materials.
- Some plants can also be harmful.

Examples highlighted in the chapter:
- cactus spines can injure people
- sap from dumbcane can cause rashes and itching
- weeds compete with useful plants for sunlight, water, and nutrients

This helps students avoid an overly simplistic idea that all plants are only helpful.

#### Where plants grow

- Plants grow in many different places, including fields, forests, gardens, ponds, and rivers.
- Some plants also grow in unusual places such as drains, roofs, walls, and on other plants.
- Plants can be classified according to where they grow: land plants and water plants.

Key distinction:
- land plants grow on land, including on soil, trees, buildings, walls, and even drains
- water plants grow in water such as lakes, ponds, and rivers

Students should understand that classification by habitat depends on where the plant grows, not on its size or appearance.

#### Classifying plants as flowering and non-flowering

- Plants can also be broadly classified into flowering plants and non-flowering plants.
- Flowering plants produce flowers.
- Non-flowering plants do not produce flowers.

Important details about flowering plants:
- examples include hibiscus, orchid, mango, chilli, and banana
- flowering plants produce flowers when they become adult plants
- the flowers can develop into fruits that contain seeds
- flowering plants may not bear flowers all the time
- some plants flower throughout the year, while others flower only at certain times of the year

Important details about non-flowering plants:
- examples include ferns and mosses

Important misconception corrected by the chapter:
- grasses are flowering plants, even though their flowers and fruits may be small and not easily noticed

#### The different parts of a plant

- A plant usually has roots, a stem, and leaves.
- Some plants also have flowers and fruits.
- These parts may look different in different plants.

Students should know both the common set of parts and that not every plant shows all parts in the same way.

#### Variation in plant parts

Roots:
- roots are usually found underground
- some plants have roots above the ground, such as mangrove trees

Stems:
- stems are usually found above the ground
- some plants have stems underground, such as the onion plant

Leaves:
- leaves of different plants have different shapes, sizes, and colours

Flowers:
- flowers are only found on flowering plants
- flowers of different plants have different shapes, sizes, and colours

Fruits:
- fruits are only found on flowering plants
- fruits differ in shape, size, colour, and taste
- some fruits are edible, while others are not

#### What students should truly master from this topic

By the end of this topic, students should be able to:
- state that plants are living things that need air, water, and food
- explain that plants can make their own food
- classify plants by where they grow into land plants and water plants
- classify plants into flowering and non-flowering groups
- identify the usual main parts of a plant: roots, stem, and leaves
- recognize that some plants also have flowers and fruits
- explain that plant parts can vary in position, shape, size, and colour across different plants

#### Common assessment traps

- saying plants are non-living because they do not move from place to place
- assuming all plants grow only in soil or gardens
- assuming flowering plants always have visible flowers all the time
- thinking grasses are non-flowering plants because their flowers are hard to notice
- assuming roots are always underground or stems are always above ground with no exceptions
- saying all fruits can be eaten

### 4. Animals

#### Big Questions / intended mastery

Students should be able to answer these clearly:
- How are animals similar or different?
- How do animals differ in size, shape, and colour?
- How do animals differ in their outer coverings?
- How do animals reproduce?
- How can animals be classified?

#### Core understanding

- There are many different kinds of animals.
- Animals may be similar or different in their appearances, their outer coverings, and the way they reproduce.
- Comparing animals helps students notice patterns that can be used for classification.

Main comparison features highlighted in this chapter:
- size
- shape
- colour
- outer covering
- mode of reproduction

Students should understand that animals can be compared in several valid ways, not only by what they look like at first glance.

#### How animals differ in size, shape, and colour

- Animals differ in size.
- Animals also differ in shape.
- Animals differ in colour, and colour can help us identify animals.

Important clarifications:
- two animals can have similar body shapes but different sizes
- some animals have only one main colour, while others have several colours on their bodies
- in some species, the male is more colourful than the female

This chapter trains students to separate different kinds of differences instead of blending them together.

#### Outer coverings and their purposes

- Some animals have outer coverings such as hair, scales, feathers, or shells over their layer of skin.
- Outer coverings protect animals and may also serve other functions.

Key functions and examples:
- hair keeps animals warm and helps protect them from injury
- scales protect animals from injury and predators
- feathers keep birds warm and help them fly
- shells protect animals from injury and predators

Important exception:
- some animals, such as frogs, octopuses, and earthworms, do not have an outer covering over their skin

#### Reproduction in animals

- Animals reproduce in different ways.
- Some animals lay eggs.
- Other animals give birth to their young.

Examples emphasized in the chapter:
- egg-laying animals include chickens, geese, crocodiles, snakes, turtles, frogs, platypuses, and spiny anteaters
- animals that give birth include cats, cows, elephants, giraffes, humans, kangaroos, and sheep

Students should treat reproduction as a major classification feature rather than a fact to memorize in isolation.

#### Other ways animals can differ

- Animals also differ in the types of food they eat.
- Animals move in different ways using different body parts.
- Animals live in different places, such as on land, in water, or both.

Useful food groupings:
- herbivores eat only plants
- carnivores eat only animals
- omnivores eat both plants and animals

Useful movement examples:
- crawling
- flying
- gliding
- hopping
- running
- swimming
- walking

#### Classifying animals into major groups

- Animals may be classified into amphibians, birds, fish, insects, mammals, and reptiles.
- Each group has characteristics that help identify it.

Amphibians:
- can live on land and in water
- have thin skin without another outer covering
- breathe through skin when it is moist or when in water
- breathe using lungs when on land
- most lay eggs and their young develop in water

Birds:
- have feathers
- have a beak, a pair of wings, and a pair of legs
- lay eggs protected by hard shells
- not all birds can fly

Fish:
- live only in water
- usually have scales, though some do not
- have fins for swimming and gills for breathing
- most lay eggs, though some give birth

Insects:
- have a hard outer covering or exoskeleton
- body is divided into head, thorax, and abdomen
- have three pairs of legs and a pair of feelers
- many lay eggs
- some have wings, while others do not

Mammals:
- most have hair on their bodies
- most give birth to their young
- young mammals feed on their mother's milk
- most live on land, though some live in the sea or spend time both on land and in water
- some mammals are herbivores, carnivores, or omnivores
- bats are mammals that can fly

Reptiles:
- have dry skin, usually covered with scales
- breathe using lungs
- some, such as turtles, have shells
- most lay eggs

#### Key distinctions students should internalize

- the feature used for classification matters: habitat, body covering, body parts, and reproduction can all lead to meaningful groupings
- the same broad rule often has exceptions, so students should avoid overgeneralizing from one example
- a named animal group is identified by a bundle of features, not by one fact alone

Important misconceptions corrected by the chapter:
- a whale is not a fish; it is a mammal because it gives birth and feeds its young with milk
- a spider is not an insect because it has eight legs and no feelers

#### What students should truly master from this topic

By the end of this topic, students should be able to:
- compare animals by size, shape, colour, outer covering, and reproduction
- identify the functions of common animal outer coverings
- distinguish animals that lay eggs from animals that give birth
- classify animals into amphibians, birds, fish, insects, mammals, and reptiles
- state key characteristics of each animal group
- use examples and exceptions carefully when deciding an animal's group

#### Common assessment traps

- classifying an animal using only where it lives without checking other characteristics
- assuming all birds can fly
- assuming all fish have scales or all fish lay eggs
- assuming every mammal lives on land
- saying whales are fish because they live in water
- saying spiders are insects because they are small and have many legs
- treating one visible feature as enough when several characteristics should be considered together

### 5. Fungi And Bacteria

#### Big Questions / intended mastery

Students should be able to answer these clearly:
- What are fungi?
- How are fungi useful or harmful to us?
- What are bacteria?
- How are bacteria useful or harmful to us?

#### Core understanding

- Fungi and bacteria are living things found in many places.
- They are not plants.
- Many fungi and bacteria are microorganisms, meaning they can only be seen clearly under a microscope.
- Both fungi and bacteria can be useful to humans, but some can also be harmful.

Students should see this chapter as widening the idea of living things beyond plants and animals.

#### What fungi are

- Fungi are a large group of living things.
- They grow in moist places.
- Common examples include mushrooms, bracket fungi, puff balls, and mould.

Important distinctions:
- fungi are classified as a different group from plants
- fungi do not have roots and leaves
- fungi cannot make their own food like plants can

Microscope-related idea:
- some fungi, such as mould and yeast, are very tiny and can only be seen clearly with a microscope
- larger fungi, such as mushrooms, are large enough to be seen without one

How fungi feed:
- fungi feed on other living things, which may be dead or alive
- they break down matter into simpler substances and absorb them

#### How fungi reproduce

- Fungi reproduce through spores.
- These spores are very tiny and light, so they can be carried easily by wind.

Examples from the chapter:
- in moulds, spores may be found in spore cases at the ends of thread-like structures
- in mushrooms, spores may be found in the gills

#### How fungi are useful or harmful

Useful fungi:
- some mushrooms can be eaten
- yeast is used to make bread, beer, and wine
- some moulds are used to make cheese
- Penicillium produces penicillin, an antibiotic used in modern medicine

Harmful fungi:
- some fungi grow on living plants and animals
- fungi on human skin can cause athlete's foot and ringworm
- moulds can spoil food and other materials such as bread, cakes, chocolates, leather goods, bags, and shoes

Students should understand that whether a fungus is described as useful or harmful depends on its effects in context.

#### What bacteria are

- Bacteria are a large group of living things that can be found in many places.
- Like fungi, bacteria feed on other living things, which may be dead or alive.
- Bacteria are microorganisms and can only be seen under a microscope.

Important misconception corrected by the chapter:
- bacteria and other microorganisms are living things, not non-living `germs`

#### How bacteria are useful or harmful

Useful bacteria:
- some bacteria are used to make cheese and yoghurt

Harmful bacteria:
- some bacteria grow on food and spoil it
- some bacteria make us ill and can cause illnesses such as diarrhoea and sore throat

#### Fungi and bacteria as decomposers

- Fungi and bacteria are important because they are decomposers.
- They usually grow on dead plants and animals and break them down into simpler substances.
- This process is called decomposition.
- Nutrients produced during decomposition can be used by plants as fertilizers.

This is a key ecological role that helps students connect microorganisms to nutrient cycling, not just disease and spoilage.

#### What students should truly master from this topic

By the end of this topic, students should be able to:
- state that fungi and bacteria are living things
- explain that fungi are not plants
- describe fungi and bacteria as microorganisms when appropriate
- explain that fungi reproduce by spores
- list useful and harmful examples of fungi and bacteria
- explain that fungi and bacteria can act as decomposers

#### Common assessment traps

- saying fungi are plants because some grow in soil
- saying fungi make their own food like plants
- assuming all fungi are harmful
- assuming all bacteria are harmful
- saying bacteria or germs are non-living
- forgetting that some fungi are large enough to be seen without a microscope while many microorganisms are not

### 6. Exploring Materials

#### Big Questions / intended mastery

Students should be able to answer these clearly:
- What are the different types of materials?
- What are some of the properties of materials?
- How do we choose the right material to make an object?

#### Core understanding

- Materials are the substances used to make the objects around us.
- There is a wide variety of materials in everyday life.
- Materials can come from natural sources such as plants, animals, and the ground.
- Some materials are made by humans.

Students should see this chapter as building a bridge between what an object is made of and why that material is suitable for that use.

#### Common materials and their sources

- Wood comes from plants, especially stems.
- Rubber comes from the sap of rubber trees.
- Cotton fabric comes from plant fibres.
- Silk and wool come from animals.
- Glass is made from sand and other materials.
- Metals such as copper, iron, and gold come from the ground.
- Ceramic is made from clay obtained from the ground.
- Many plastics are made from chemicals obtained from petroleum.

Useful applications highlighted in the chapter:
- wood for boats, furniture, and houses
- rubber for balloons, erasers, gloves, and toys
- cotton and silk for clothes and thread or accessories
- glass for window panes, lenses, and vases
- metal for cars, jewellery, utensils, tools, and wires
- ceramic for cups, plates, and pots
- plastics for ropes, windbreakers, containers, bottles, and packing materials

#### Important material properties

The chapter emphasizes these properties:
- strength
- flexibility
- ability to float or sink in water
- waterproofness
- transparency

Students should learn both what each property means and how it affects the uses of the material.

#### Meaning of each property

Strength:
- strength is the ability to support a heavy load without breaking or tearing
- strong materials resist breaking better than weak materials

Flexibility:
- flexibility is the ability to bend without breaking
- flexible materials bend more easily than stiff materials

Float or sink:
- some materials or objects float in water
- others sink in water

Waterproof or non-waterproof:
- waterproof materials do not absorb water
- non-waterproof materials absorb water

Transparency:
- transparency is the ability to allow light to pass through
- some materials allow most light to pass through
- some allow only some light to pass through
- some do not allow light to pass through

#### Testing material properties

Students should also understand the simple testing ideas in the chapter:
- strength can be tested by trying to break or tear the material
- flexibility can be tested by trying to bend the material
- floating or sinking can be tested by placing the object in water
- waterproofness can be tested by dipping the material in water or pouring water over it
- transparency can be tested by shining light through the material and observing how much passes through

This helps students treat properties as observable and testable, not just as vocabulary words.

#### Choosing the right material for an object

- We use different materials to make different objects.
- Some materials have properties that make them suitable for certain uses.
- To choose the right material, we need to consider both the properties of the material and the use of the object.

Examples emphasized in the chapter:
- nails are made of steel because steel is strong
- water bottles are made of plastic because plastic is waterproof
- if an object must be strong and allow most light to pass through, glass may be a suitable choice

#### One object, many possible materials

- Different materials can sometimes be used to make the same object.
- For example, a cup can be made from ceramic, glass, or plastic.

Students should not assume that there is always only one possible correct material. The best choice depends on which properties matter most.

#### Different parts of one object may use different materials

- A single material may not have all the required properties for an entire object.
- Different parts of the same object may therefore be made from different materials.

Example from the chapter:
- a bicycle frame is made of metal because it needs strength
- a bicycle lamp may use clear plastic because it should allow most light to pass through
- a bicycle tyre is made of rubber because it needs flexibility

#### Variety within plastics

- Different types of plastic are used to make different objects.
- Flexible plastic can be used for straws and raincoats.
- Stiff plastic can be used for plates and fan blades.
- Plastic that allows most light to pass through can be used for goggles and spectacle lenses.
- Plastic that allows some light to pass through can be used for shower screen doors and sunglasses.
- Plastic that does not allow light to pass through can be used for toilet doors and bags.

This helps students avoid treating `plastic` as one uniform material with one fixed set of properties.

#### Everyday and environmental context

- People use available materials in different environments to build useful objects and shelters.
- The chapter's daily-life examples show that unusual homes may be made from grass, sticks, mud, cow dung, animal skin, snow, or ice depending on local conditions.

This reinforces the bigger idea that material choice depends on what is available and what properties are needed.

#### What students should truly master from this topic

By the end of this topic, students should be able to:
- identify common materials and state their sources
- compare materials using properties such as strength, flexibility, waterproofness, floating or sinking, and transparency
- explain how simple tests can be used to investigate material properties
- relate the properties of materials to their uses
- choose a suitable material for an object or a part of an object and justify the choice
- recognize that more than one material may sometimes be suitable for the same object

#### Common assessment traps

- naming an object's material without explaining which property makes it suitable
- confusing the source of a material with the object made from it
- saying all plastics have exactly the same properties
- treating transparent, translucent, and opaque ideas as the same
- assuming one object can only be made from one material
- forgetting that different parts of the same object may need different materials

## Systems

### Theme understanding

`Systems` is about understanding how parts work together to perform functions that a single part cannot carry out alone.

Across chapters 14 to 22, students should build these big ideas:
- a system is made up of different parts with specific roles
- the parts of a living or non-living system are interconnected and depend on one another
- materials and substances often need to be transported through a system to where they are needed
- failure in one part of a system can affect how the whole system works
- structure and function are linked: the design of a part helps explain what it does

Students should gradually move beyond naming parts. They should learn to explain how matter, energy, or signals move through a system, and why the arrangement of parts matters.

Common cross-chapter habits of thinking in this theme:
- identify the main function of the system before memorizing its parts
- trace the pathway of movement through the system step by step
- relate each part to its role in the whole system
- compare similar systems across humans, plants, and electrical circuits
- watch for breakdown points, because system failure often comes from one missing or damaged part

This theme is a major bridge from simple classification to process-based science. It helps students reason about coordinated action, flow, and interdependence.

### 14. The Human Body As A System

#### Big Questions / intended mastery

Students should be able to answer these clearly:
- What is a system?
- What are some organ systems in the human body?
- How do the various organ systems work together?

#### Core understanding

- A system is made up of two or more parts that function together.
- If one part is damaged or does not work properly, the system cannot function properly.
- Systems can be man-made or natural.
- Living things are natural systems made of parts with special functions that work together to keep the organism alive and healthy.

#### Organ systems in the human body

- The human body is made up of different organ systems.
- Each organ system is made up of different parts and carries out different functions.

Main organ systems introduced in this chapter:
- digestive system: breaks food down, absorbs digested food, nutrients, minerals, and water, and stores undigested food until removal
- respiratory system: takes in oxygen and removes carbon dioxide
- circulatory system: transports oxygen, digested food, nutrients, minerals, water, and waste materials through the body
- skeletal system: supports the body, gives it shape, and protects delicate organs
- muscular system: enables different parts of the body to move

#### How organ systems work together

- Our organ systems are interconnected.
- They work together so the body can function properly.
- The digestive system provides digested food, the circulatory system carries it around the body, and the respiratory system helps remove carbon dioxide produced by body processes.
- If one system fails, other systems will be affected as well.

#### What students should truly master from this topic

By the end of this topic, students should be able to:
- define a system as parts working together
- identify several human organ systems and their basic functions
- explain that organ systems depend on one another
- describe simple examples of cooperation between digestive, circulatory, and respiratory systems

#### Common assessment traps

- treating a system as just a collection of parts rather than parts working together
- listing organ systems without linking them to their functions
- assuming one body system can work independently of all the others

### 15. The Digestive System

#### Big Questions / intended mastery

Students should be able to answer these clearly:
- Why is digestion important?
- How is food digested?
- How do the different organs in the digestive system work together?

#### Core understanding

- We eat food to obtain energy and to allow the body's systems to function properly.
- Food must be broken down into simpler substances before it can be absorbed into the blood and carried to different parts of the body.
- Digestion is the process by which food is broken down into simpler substances.

#### Main organs of the digestive system

- The digestive system consists of the mouth, gullet, stomach, small intestine, large intestine, and anus.
- Digestive juices in some organs help break food down into simpler substances the body can absorb.

#### How digestion happens

Mouth:
- digestion begins in the mouth
- teeth chew food into smaller pieces
- saliva softens food and begins digestion
- the tongue mixes food with saliva and rolls it into small balls for swallowing

Gullet:
- the gullet is a tube that connects the mouth to the stomach
- muscles in the gullet push swallowed food down to the stomach

Stomach:
- the stomach is a muscular bag that produces digestive juices
- food is squeezed and mixed with these juices
- some food is broken down into simpler substances here

Small intestine:
- digestion is completed in the small intestine
- digested food and other nutrients pass through the walls of the small intestine into the blood

Large intestine and anus:
- water and minerals are absorbed from undigested food in the large intestine
- the remaining undigested food becomes waste material
- waste is removed through the anus

#### How the digestive system works as a system

- Each organ has a role to play.
- The organs must work together for digestion and absorption to occur successfully.
- If one organ does not function properly, food may not be properly digested or absorbed, affecting the rest of the body.

Important distinction:
- food is not digested in every part of the digestive system
- the main digestion happens in the mouth, stomach, and small intestine

#### What students should truly master from this topic

By the end of this topic, students should be able to:
- explain why food needs to be digested
- identify the major organs of the digestive system
- describe the pathway food takes through the body
- explain the roles of the mouth, gullet, stomach, small intestine, large intestine, and anus
- explain that digestion and absorption require organs to work together

#### Common assessment traps

- saying digestion happens in every organ of the digestive system
- mixing up digestion with absorption
- forgetting that the large intestine mainly absorbs water and minerals rather than digests food
- treating the digestive system as a simple tube without different functions in different parts

### 16. Plants And Their Parts

#### Big Questions / intended mastery

Students should be able to answer these clearly:
- What are the parts of a plant?
- What are the functions of each part of the plant?

#### Core understanding

- Plants are living things made of many parts.
- The basic plant parts focused on in this chapter are roots, stem, and leaves.
- Flowers and fruits are produced by adult flowering plants.

Useful plant-system framing:
- a plant can be divided into the root system and the shoot system
- the shoot system includes the stem, branches, leaves, flowers, and fruits

#### Roots

- Roots of most plants are found under the ground.
- Roots hold the plant firmly to the soil.
- Roots absorb water and minerals from the soil.

Types and modifications highlighted in the chapter:
- tap root system
- fibrous root system
- storage roots
- breathing or respiratory roots
- clasping roots
- prop roots

Students should understand that roots can have specialized forms and functions, not just one standard appearance.

#### Stem

- The stem of most plants grows above the ground.
- The stem supports the plant.
- It holds up the leaves so they can trap sunlight for making food.
- Some plants have strong upright stems, while others have weak stems that climb or creep.
- Some stems are underground and may store food, such as potato and ginger.

#### Leaves

- Leaves make food for the plant.
- Leaves trap sunlight in order to make food.
- Most leaves are green because they contain chlorophyll.
- Leaves have tiny openings, mostly on the underside, that allow gases to move in and out.
- Most leaves have a leaf stalk, leaf blade, and veins.

Important correction:
- insect-eating plants such as the Venus flytrap still make their own food; catching insects helps them obtain nutrients, not replace photosynthesis

#### Summary of functions

- root: holds plant firmly to the soil and absorbs water and minerals
- stem: supports branches and leaves
- leaf: makes food and allows gases to move in and out

#### What students should truly master from this topic

By the end of this topic, students should be able to:
- identify the main parts of a plant
- describe the functions of roots, stems, and leaves
- recognize that roots and stems can be modified for special functions
- explain that leaves make food and also allow gas exchange

#### Common assessment traps

- assuming all roots and stems have the same form or position
- saying leaves only make food and ignoring gas exchange
- saying insect-eating plants do not make their own food
- treating flowers and fruits as always present on all plants

### 17. The Plant Transport System

#### Big Questions / intended mastery

Students should be able to answer these clearly:
- What is the plant transport system?
- How does the plant transport system work?

#### Core understanding

- Food made by leaves is transported to other parts of the plant such as roots, stem, flowers, and fruits.
- Water and minerals absorbed by roots are also transported to other parts of the plant.
- The plant transport system carries these substances through the plant.
- The system is made up of two types of tubes.

#### The two types of transport tubes

- water-carrying tubes (`xylem`) run from roots to other parts of the plant
- food-carrying tubes (`phloem`) run from leaves to other parts of the plant

Key functions:
- xylem transports water and minerals and also helps support the plant
- phloem transports food made in the leaves

#### How the plant transport system works

- Water-carrying and food-carrying tubes work at the same time.
- Water and minerals move upward from the roots through the stem to other plant parts.
- Food made in the leaves moves to other parts of the plant for life processes.

Important correction:
- these transport tubes are not found only in the stem
- they are also found in leaves, flowers, and roots so materials can reach all parts of the plant

#### Helpful observation examples

- In a celery stalk placed in coloured water, the coloured water moves up through the water-carrying tubes.
- The rings seen in a tree stump are the remains of old water-carrying tubes.

#### What students should truly master from this topic

By the end of this topic, students should be able to:
- define the plant transport system
- distinguish between xylem and phloem
- describe what each type of tube transports
- explain that plant transport occurs throughout the whole plant, not only in the stem

#### Common assessment traps

- mixing up the direction of transport in xylem and phloem
- saying food is made in the roots rather than the leaves
- saying the transport system exists only in the stem
- forgetting that xylem also helps support the plant

### 18. Air And The Respiratory System

#### Big Questions / intended mastery

Students should be able to answer these clearly:
- What are the components of air?
- What are the main differences between breathing and respiration?
- What are the functions of the organs in the respiratory system?
- How do the organs in the respiratory system work together?
- How do animals and plants exchange gases with their surroundings?

#### Components of air

- Air is a mixture of gases in the atmosphere.
- Air is made up of about `78%` nitrogen, about `21%` oxygen, about `0.03%` carbon dioxide, and variable amounts of water vapour and other gases.

Main roles of key components:
- oxygen is needed by living things for respiration and also supports burning
- nitrogen is the most abundant gas and does not support burning
- carbon dioxide is produced during respiration, burning, and decay, and is used by plants in photosynthesis
- water vapour is important in the water cycle and weather

#### Breathing and respiration

- Breathing is the physical act of taking in and giving out air.
- Respiration is the process in cells where oxygen and nutrients are used to release energy, producing carbon dioxide.

Students should keep this distinction clear:
- breathing happens through organs such as the nose, windpipe, lungs, and diaphragm
- respiration happens in all living cells

#### Parts of the respiratory system and their functions

- nose: allows air to enter and leave, traps dirt with hair and mucus, and warms and moistens air
- windpipe: carries air from the nose to the air tubes
- air tubes: branch from the windpipe and transport air to the lungs
- lungs: contain air sacs and allow gas exchange
- air sacs: have a rich supply of blood vessels and provide a large surface area for gas exchange
- diaphragm: a dome-shaped muscle important for breathing

#### How the respiratory system works

Breathing in:
- ribs move out and upwards
- diaphragm moves downwards
- chest expands
- air enters through the nose and mouth and moves into the lungs

In the lungs:
- oxygen passes from air sacs into blood vessels
- carbon dioxide passes from blood vessels into air sacs

Breathing out:
- ribs move in and downwards
- diaphragm moves upwards
- chest becomes smaller
- air is forced out of the lungs

#### Gas exchange in plants and animals

- insects and arachnids use breathing tubes
- fish take in oxygen dissolved in water through gills
- birds and mammals use lungs
- amphibians may use lungs, skin, or gills depending on type and stage
- plants exchange gases through tiny openings called stomata, mostly on the underside of leaves

Important correction:
- plants respire all the time, not only at night

#### What students should truly master from this topic

By the end of this topic, students should be able to:
- name the main components of air
- explain the difference between breathing and respiration
- identify the organs of the respiratory system and their functions
- describe how breathing in and breathing out happen
- explain simple gas exchange in animals and plants

#### Common assessment traps

- treating breathing and respiration as the same process
- saying nitrogen supports burning
- saying plants respire only when they are not photosynthesizing
- mixing up where oxygen and carbon dioxide move during gas exchange

### 19. The Circulatory System

#### Big Questions / intended mastery

Students should be able to answer these clearly:
- What is the circulatory system?
- What are the parts of the circulatory system?
- How do the organs in the circulatory system work together?
- How do other organ systems work together with the circulatory system?
- What are the differences between the transport systems in animals and plants?

#### Core understanding

- The circulatory system is made up of the heart, blood vessels, and blood.
- It transports oxygen, digested food, and water to all parts of the body.
- It also carries carbon dioxide and other waste materials away from different parts of the body.

#### Parts of the circulatory system

Heart:
- a muscular four-chambered organ that pumps blood around the body
- each heartbeat is a cycle of contraction and relaxation
- pulse is the pressure wave produced in blood vessels with each heartbeat
- pulse rate increases during exercise because the body needs more oxygen and digested food

Blood vessels:
- tubes through which blood flows
- arteries generally carry oxygen-rich blood from the heart to the body
- veins generally carry oxygen-poor blood from the body back to the heart
- capillaries are tiny vessels where exchange with body cells occurs

Blood:
- the transport medium in the body
- carries digested food, oxygen, water, carbon dioxide, and other waste materials
- contains red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets

#### How the circulatory system works as a system

- The heart pumps oxygen-rich blood to the body through arteries.
- Digested food, oxygen, and water pass through capillary walls into cells.
- Cells use these materials and produce carbon dioxide and other waste materials.
- Waste materials pass back into capillaries.
- Veins carry blood and wastes back toward the heart.
- Carbon dioxide-rich blood is returned to the heart and sent to the lungs.

#### How the circulatory system works with other systems

- It works with the respiratory system by carrying oxygen from the lungs and removing carbon dioxide
- It works with the digestive system by transporting digested nutrients
- It works with the muscular system because heart muscle pumps blood and smooth muscles in vessel walls help move blood

#### Comparing animal and plant transport systems

Animals:
- transport oxygen, carbon dioxide, digested food, and water
- use blood
- have arteries, veins, and capillaries
- have a heart to pump the liquid

Plants:
- transport water and minerals from roots and food from leaves
- use sap
- have xylem and phloem
- do not have a pumping organ like a heart

#### What students should truly master from this topic

By the end of this topic, students should be able to:
- identify the heart, blood vessels, and blood as parts of the circulatory system
- explain the roles of arteries, veins, and capillaries
- describe what blood transports
- explain how the circulatory system links with respiratory and digestive systems
- compare animal and plant transport systems

#### Common assessment traps

- saying arteries and veins have the same function
- confusing pulse with heartbeat without linking pulse to blood movement
- forgetting that capillaries are the exchange sites
- mixing up plant sap and human blood as if they were the same transport medium

### 20. The Unit Of Life

#### Big Questions / intended mastery

Students should be able to answer these clearly:
- What is a cell?
- What are the different parts of animal and plant cells?
- What are the functions of the different parts of a cell?

#### Core understanding

- All living things are made of one or more cells.
- A cell is the smallest unit of life.
- Cells can grow, take in food, reproduce, and respond to changes in the environment.
- Organisms may be unicellular or multicellular.

Useful organization idea:
- cells form tissues
- tissues form organs
- organs connect in systems

#### Microscopes and seeing cells

- Most cells are too small to be seen with the naked eye.
- A microscope helps us see very small objects.
- The common light microscope uses lenses to magnify the specimen.

#### Parts of animal and plant cells

Animal cell:
- nucleus
- cytoplasm
- cell membrane
- no chloroplasts
- no cell wall

Plant cell:
- nucleus
- cytoplasm
- cell membrane
- chloroplasts
- cell wall

#### Functions of cell parts

- cytoplasm: jelly-like substance that allows substances to move around within the cell
- nucleus: controls what happens in the cell and contains information passed from one generation to another
- cell membrane: controls movement of materials into and out of the cell
- cell wall: supports the plant cell and gives it shape
- chloroplast: contains chlorophyll and captures sunlight for plants to make food

Important corrections:
- not all plant cells contain chloroplasts
- not all cells contain a nucleus, for example human red blood cells

#### Cell division, growth, and reproduction

- Cells increase in number by cell division.
- New cells replace dead or damaged cells.
- Cell division helps organisms grow and reproduce.
- In bacteria, one cell divides into two daughter cells.
- In yeast, daughter cells may form by budding.

#### What students should truly master from this topic

By the end of this topic, students should be able to:
- define a cell as the smallest unit of life
- explain that living things are made of cells
- identify similarities and differences between plant and animal cells
- state the functions of nucleus, cytoplasm, cell membrane, cell wall, and chloroplast
- relate cell division to growth and reproduction

#### Common assessment traps

- saying all plant cells have chloroplasts
- saying all cells have nuclei
- mixing up cell membrane and cell wall
- treating cells, tissues, organs, and systems as interchangeable levels

### 21. Electrical Systems

#### Big Questions / intended mastery

Students should be able to answer these clearly:
- What is an electrical system?
- What is a simple electric circuit made up of?
- How does electric current flow in a circuit?
- What are electrical conductors and insulators?
- How do we draw circuit diagrams?

#### Core understanding

- Electricity is a form of energy.
- In electrical equipment, electricity may be used to produce light, heat, and other forms of energy.
- The path through which electricity flows is the electric circuit.
- An electrical system is made up of different components that work together.

#### Components of a simple electric circuit

- energy source such as a battery
- wires
- bulb
- switch

Important component ideas:
- a battery has positive and negative ends; in a closed circuit, chemical reactions in the battery produce electricity
- wires are usually made of copper and covered with materials such as plastic or rubber
- a switch controls the flow of electric current by opening or closing the circuit
- a bulb gives light when current passes through its filament and heats it up

#### How electric current flows

- Electric current flows only in a closed or complete circuit.
- When there is a gap or break, the circuit is open and current cannot flow.
- A bulb lights only when its metal tip and metal casing are connected to different ends of the battery through a complete circuit.
- If too much current flows, the filament may melt and the bulb will blow.

Important correction:
- the book states electron flow is from the negative to the positive end of a battery

#### Series and parallel circuits

Series circuits:
- have only one path for current to flow
- if one bulb is removed or blows, the circuit stops working

Parallel circuits:
- have more than one path for current to flow
- one bulb can still work when another is removed or blows
- home wiring is commonly arranged in parallel

#### Conductors and insulators

- conductors allow electric current to flow through them
- most metals are good conductors, for example copper and silver
- some liquids such as salt solution and mercury also conduct electricity
- insulators do not allow electric current to flow through them
- many non-metals such as glass, air, plastic, wood, clay, cloth, paper, and cotton are insulators

Important correction:
- not all non-metallic materials are insulators; graphite is a non-metal that conducts electricity

#### Circuit tester and circuit diagrams

- A circuit tester helps distinguish between conductors and insulators.
- If the tested object closes the gap and current flows, the bulb lights up.
- Circuit diagrams use symbols to represent components such as batteries, switches, bulbs, and wires.

#### What students should truly master from this topic

By the end of this topic, students should be able to:
- define an electrical system and a simple circuit
- identify the main components of a simple circuit
- explain the difference between open and closed circuits
- compare series and parallel circuits
- distinguish conductors from insulators
- read or draw simple circuit diagrams using standard symbols

#### Common assessment traps

- saying a battery stores electricity rather than chemicals that can produce electricity
- assuming any connection between bulb and battery will light the bulb
- forgetting that series circuits fail when one component breaks
- assuming all non-metals are insulators
- mixing up real components with circuit symbols

### 22. Using Electricity

#### Big Questions / intended mastery

Students should be able to answer these clearly:
- What are some variables that can affect electric current in a circuit?
- Why should we use electricity safely?
- How can we use electricity wisely?

#### Variables that affect brightness in a circuit

- The brightness of a bulb depends on the arrangement of batteries, the number of batteries, the number of bulbs, and the arrangement of bulbs.

Batteries in series and parallel:
- batteries in series provide more power, so a bulb can shine more brightly
- batteries in parallel last longer but provide less power than the same batteries connected in series

Number of batteries:
- when more batteries are connected in series, bulbs shine more brightly
- too many batteries in series may cause too much current and blow the bulb

Number and arrangement of bulbs:
- when more bulbs are added in series, each bulb shines less brightly
- if one bulb in a series circuit blows, the others do not light up
- bulbs in parallel can each shine as brightly as before because current has separate paths
- if one bulb in a parallel circuit blows, the others can still light up

#### Using electricity safely

- Electricity can be dangerous.
- It can cause fires and electric shocks.
- Common causes of accidents include wet hands, damaged insulation, and overloading power points.

Safe-use habits emphasized in the chapter:
- do not connect too many appliances to one socket
- make sure wires are not exposed
- never touch switches with wet hands
- use circuit breakers and fuses
- do not repair electrical equipment yourself

Important correction:
- it is not always safe to touch internal parts of an appliance even when it is switched off, because some components such as capacitors can still hold charge

#### Using electricity wisely

- We should conserve electricity because it is often generated using natural resources such as coal, oil, and natural gas.
- Conserving electricity also saves money.

Examples from the chapter:
- turn off appliances when not in use
- use power-saving features
- use energy-saving bulbs
- use a small lamp rather than bright lights when appropriate
- do not leave the refrigerator door open for long
- use a fan instead of an air-conditioner when suitable

#### How electricity is generated

- In many power stations, another source of energy is used to generate electricity.
- In Singapore, fossil fuels are commonly burned to boil water, produce steam, turn turbines, and drive generators.
- Other sources shown in the chapter include wind, running water, and nuclear energy.

#### What students should truly master from this topic

By the end of this topic, students should be able to:
- explain how battery arrangement, number of batteries, and number or arrangement of bulbs affect brightness
- compare bulbs in series and parallel
- explain why electricity must be used safely
- list practical ways to use electricity safely and wisely
- explain in simple terms that electricity is generated by converting other forms of energy

#### Common assessment traps

- assuming batteries in parallel always make bulbs brighter than batteries in series
- forgetting that adding more bulbs in series reduces brightness
- saying series and parallel bulb arrangements behave the same when one bulb blows
- treating electricity conservation as only about money rather than also about natural resources
- overlooking overload, wet hands, or damaged insulation as safety hazards

### 24. Forces

#### Big Questions / intended mastery

Students should be able to answer these clearly:
- What is a force?
- What are the effects of a force?
- What are the different types of forces around us?
- What is friction?
- How does friction affect us in our daily lives?
- How can we reduce friction?
- What is elastic spring force?
- What is gravitational force?
- What is magnetic force?

#### Core understanding

- A force is a push or a pull exerted by one object on another.
- Students should recognize that a push is applied away from the body and a pull is applied towards the body.
- Forces help us do work in daily life, but not all forces are helpful in every situation.

#### Effects of a force

A force can:
- start a stationary object moving
- stop a moving object
- make an object move faster
- make an object move slower
- change the direction of a moving object
- change the shape or size of an object

Students should treat these effects as the main observable evidence of a force.

#### Types of forces in the chapter

The chapter groups forces into:
- contact forces
  Examples: frictional force, elastic spring force
- forces acting at a distance
  Examples: gravitational force, magnetic force

#### Friction

- Friction is a force between surfaces rubbing against each other.
- Friction opposes motion.
- Friction acts in the direction opposite to the motion of the object.
- Friction can produce heat.

Useful effects of friction:
- allows us to walk and run without slipping
- allows tyres to grip the road
- allows brakes to slow vehicles
- can produce enough heat to light a match

Problems caused by friction:
- wear and tear on surfaces
- overheating in machines
- objects moving more slowly than desired

Ways to reduce friction:
- use lubricants such as oil or grease
- use rollers, wheels, or ball bearings
- use streamlined body shapes for movement through air or water

Key distinction students should grasp:
- friction is often useful for grip and control
- but friction is undesirable when it causes unnecessary resistance, wear, or heating

#### Elastic spring force

- Elastic objects can change shape when a force acts on them and return to their original shape when the force is removed.
- Springs and rubber bands are examples of elastic objects.
- Pulling on a spring stretches it.
- Pushing on a spring compresses it.
- The larger the force, the more the spring is stretched or compressed.
- A stretched or compressed spring exerts an elastic spring force that tends to restore its original shape.

Daily-life examples:
- mattresses
- clothes pegs
- spring balances

#### Gravitational force

- Gravity is a force of attraction between objects that have mass.
- Gravitational force increases when mass increases.
- Gravitational force decreases when distance between objects increases.
- When students talk about an object's gravity on Earth in everyday primary science, they usually mean the pull between the object and Earth.

Weight and mass:
- weight is the amount of gravitational force acting on an object
- mass is the amount of matter in an object
- weight is a force
- mass is not a force
- mass stays the same in different places
- weight can change if the gravitational force changes

Common misconception to correct:
- weight is not the same as mass

#### Balanced and unbalanced forces

- If forces are equal in size and act in opposite directions, they cancel each other.
- In that case, there is no change in motion caused by the forces.
- If forces are not balanced, the object may move, speed up, slow down, or change direction.

Useful examples:
- tug-of-war with equal pull on both sides
- hot air balloon moving up, down, or staying level depending on the balance between upward push and gravity

#### Magnetic force

- Magnetic force is exerted by magnets on magnetic materials.
- Magnetic materials include iron, steel, and nickel.
- Non-magnetic materials include rubber, wood, and plastic.
- Magnets can attract magnetic materials.
- Unlike poles attract.
- Like poles repel.

Two magnetic-force ideas students should know:
- magnetic force of attraction
- magnetic force of repulsion

#### What students should truly master from this topic

By the end of this topic, students should be able to:
- identify whether a situation involves a push or a pull
- describe the effect of a force in a given scenario
- classify common forces as contact or at-a-distance
- explain when friction is useful and when it is a problem
- suggest valid ways to reduce friction
- explain stretching and compression in terms of elastic spring force
- distinguish clearly between weight and mass
- explain gravity as an attractive force linked to mass and distance
- apply the rules of magnetic attraction and repulsion

#### Common assessment traps

- saying all forces help us do useful work
- confusing friction with only moving objects and ignoring stationary contact cases
- mixing up weight and mass
- assuming magnetic force only acts between magnets and not between magnets and magnetic materials
- forgetting that friction opposes motion rather than always stopping motion immediately
